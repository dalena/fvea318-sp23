Critique Guidelines

Critiques are a dynamic way of gaining perspective on our works as a group that energize us to move forward in our creative process. Being actively aware that we are doing a critique helps to sharpen how a work affects us, expands our vocabulary to talk about our work, and gives us ideas of how to improve our past, current, and future work.


"Often times I find myself walking away from critiques feeling better informed about my own work, motivated to keep working on my piece, or even inspired to start a new project based on the conversations had. I see critiques not necessarily just vessels for either validation or harsh, unwanted criticism, but as opportunities for constructive criticism to help elevate the works of myself and others."[x] Robin


1. Ask the creator if they would like to contextualize their work before viewing it.
  • Some peers may want to give context before either viewing their work. Contextualizing the piece may set a direction for specific feedback they have in mind. "I find myself wanting to give a few words of direction initially so that I can gain feedback in areas that I find useful. This also helps fill in context for the audience" [x] Alice . Only remember, this is NOT an opportunity to belittle your work. Respect the time and effort that went into your project. Respect your peers to form their own readings of the work.
  • While contextualizing your work may help gain specific feedback, it is often more helpful to not contextualize the work before viewing it (unless for a trigger warning). There is time afterwards to add context but a first impression happens only once. "When I had my video class, my professor let the class speak and discuss first without any explanation from the artist. Without my own intention, the class is more active, and their responses always surprise me by seeing my work in different ways." [x] Ziying
2. The class does a cold read.
  • What do you see? What do you hear? Start with the obvious and work into the details.
  • Cold reads help to establish a common reality and impression of the work from a formal level. This allows us to develop the same ground as a group to question what the work is doing, generating, or producing. What we call "cold read", Leeman calls “obvious, verifiable observations about the object.”
  • Remarks on observable traits of a work helps us all build a working context and common ground to deepen our interpretation and discussions for the work at hand. "If we skip looking at the obvious qualities first, we start to pick and choose visual cues." [x] Lindsay "[Cold reads], especially during a group session, helps align everybody on the same page of what is being seen, before exclaiming how it is being interpreted."[x] Anna
  • Assume what you see is intended. By seeing a work as fully intended, we remove the pressure of investigation in order to be more intimate with the work. "When you assume intention, you give the artist a respect they can grow into," as Leeman says.
3. Open up the critique to questions and comments
  • Try to avoid art direction. If you think the work could improve, try to form it into a question. For example, instead of “I wish this piece included diverse clips from different movies…” you can ask “why did you choose to include clips from this specific source?”
  • Try to avoid the unborn sibling / comparing a work to something else. "This reminds me of..."" is often gratifying and useful for students getting technically acquainted with their own work but it can often deflect from the work into a space where we read the work only by relationship to something else/other/outside. "I think sometimes, if not elaborated on, associations can make an artist feel like their work is being viewed as inherently derivative" [x] Robin
Questions & thoughts to keep in mind:
  • What stood out first? What did you see as the most important or glaring feature? [x] Son
  • What questions came up? What emotions did you feel? Where do your thoughts go?
  • What themes were communicated to you?[x] Lindsay
  • If the artist asks, can you elaborate on craftsmanship? Is there room for improvement?[x] Serena
  • What kind of changes would you make to your work if you could?
  • What were the complexities of the work?
  • What part of the process was rewarding for you?
  • Did you learn anything about yourself or your practice while working on this project?
  • What were you thinking when you were planning the piece and how did it change after? Why?
  • Why did you choose to do X or include Y?
  • What method did you use and what technical aspects did you use to achieve X effect?
  • What direction will you go with this work moving forward?
